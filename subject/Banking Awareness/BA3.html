Types of Banks in India

Understanding the banking structure regulated by RBI

Banking Structure Overview

The Reserve Bank of India (RBI) classifies banks into different categories based on their functions, ownership, and regulatory requirements. This classification helps in maintaining financial stability and ensuring specialized services for different sectors of the economy.

Key Points About This Structure

  • Scheduled Banks are included in RBI's second schedule and must maintain a minimum capital requirement
  • Non-Scheduled Banks are not listed in RBI's second schedule and have different capital requirements
  • Commercial Banks focus on profit and serve all customer segments
  • Co-operative Banks serve specific communities with focus on agriculture and rural development

Scheduled Banks

Banks included in the Second Schedule of the RBI Act, 1934 with paid-up capital and reserves of at least ₹5 lakhs.

Characteristics

  • Included in RBI's Second Schedule
  • Eligible for loans from RBI at bank rate
  • Become members of clearing house
  • Subject to CRR and SLR requirements

Requirements

  • Minimum paid-up capital: ₹5 lakhs
  • Must satisfy RBI about operations
  • Must not conduct business detrimental to depositors
  • Must comply with RBI regulations

Privileges

  • Access to RBI refinance facilities
  • Membership in clearing houses
  • Right to currency chest facility
  • Inclusion in deposit insurance

Scheduled vs Non-Scheduled Banks

Feature Scheduled Banks Non-Scheduled Banks
Inclusion in RBI Act Listed in Second Schedule Not listed
Capital Requirement ₹5 lakhs minimum No fixed requirement
RBI Facilities Eligible for loans/refinance Not eligible
Clearing House Can become member Cannot become member
Regulation Strict RBI supervision Less stringent

Commercial Banks

Profit-oriented banks that accept deposits and provide loans to individuals and businesses. They form the core of India's banking system.

Public Sector Banks (PSBs)

  • Majority stake held by government
  • Examples: SBI, PNB, Bank of Baroda
  • Focus on social banking
  • 28 PSBs in India (after mergers)

Private Sector Banks

  • Majority stake held by private entities
  • Examples: HDFC, ICICI, Axis Bank
  • More tech-savvy and customer-focused
  • 22 private banks in India

Regional Rural Banks (RRBs)

  • Established under RRB Act 1976
  • Jointly owned by Central Govt, State Govt & Sponsor Bank
  • Focus on rural and agricultural credit
  • 43 RRBs operating in India

Foreign Banks

  • Headquartered in foreign country
  • Examples: Citibank, Standard Chartered
  • Operate as branches in India
  • 46 foreign banks in India

Payment Banks

  • Can accept deposits (up to ₹2 lakh)
  • Cannot issue loans or credit cards
  • Examples: Paytm Payments Bank, Airtel Payments Bank
  • 6 payment banks operational

Small Finance Banks

  • Target unserved and underserved sections
  • Examples: Ujjivan SFB, Equitas SFB
  • 75% loans to priority sectors
  • 10 small finance banks operational

Co-operative Banks

Customer-owned financial institutions that operate on cooperative principles with focus on local communities and agriculture.

Urban Co-operative Banks

  • Registered under State Co-operative Societies Act
  • Operate in urban and semi-urban areas
  • Examples: Saraswat Co-op Bank, Cosmos Bank
  • Regulated by RBI and State Govt

Rural Co-operative Banks

  • Three-tier structure: State, District, Village level
  • Focus on agricultural credit
  • Examples: State Co-op Banks, District Central Co-op Banks
  • Regulated by NABARD and State Govt

Primary Agricultural Credit Societies (PACS)

  • Grassroot level institutions
  • Direct interface with farmers
  • Over 95,000 PACS in India
  • Provide short-term crop loans

Commercial vs Co-operative Banks

Parameter Commercial Banks Co-operative Banks
Ownership Shareholders Members/Customers
Regulation RBI RBI + State Govt + NABARD
Area of Operation Nationwide/International Local/State level
Primary Focus Profit Service to members
Borrowing Power From RBI/Market From higher co-op banks
