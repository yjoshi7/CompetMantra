Capital Market in India

Understanding the market for long-term funds and securities

Capital Market Overview

The capital market is a financial market for buying and selling long-term debt or equity-backed securities. It channels savings and investments between suppliers of capital (such as retail investors and institutional investors) and those who need capital (like businesses, governments, and individuals).

Structure of Indian Capital Market

The Indian capital market is broadly divided into two main segments:

Primary Market

Also known as the new issues market, it deals with the issuance of new securities by companies, governments, or public institutions to raise fresh capital.

  • Initial Public Offerings (IPOs)
  • Follow-on Public Offers (FPOs)
  • Rights Issues
  • Private Placements

Secondary Market

Also known as the stock market, it provides a platform for trading of existing securities among investors.

  • Stock Exchanges (NSE, BSE)
  • Equity Trading
  • Debt Trading
  • Derivatives Trading

Capital Market Instruments

Various financial instruments are traded in the capital market to meet different investment needs and risk profiles:

Equity Shares

Represent ownership in a company and entitle holders to dividends and voting rights.

  • Common stock
  • Preferred stock
  • Bonus shares
  • Sweat equity shares

Debt Instruments

Fixed income instruments representing loans made by investors to borrowers.

  • Corporate bonds
  • Government securities
  • Debentures
  • Zero-coupon bonds

Derivatives

Financial contracts whose value is derived from an underlying asset.

  • Futures contracts
  • Options
  • Warrants
  • Swaps

Mutual Funds

Pooled investment vehicles that collect money from many investors to invest in securities.

  • Equity funds
  • Debt funds
  • Hybrid funds
  • Index funds

Regulatory Framework

The Indian capital market is regulated by various authorities to ensure transparency, protect investors, and promote market development:

Securities and Exchange Board of India (SEBI)

The primary regulator for the securities market in India, established in 1992 and given statutory powers through the SEBI Act, 1992.

Key Functions:

  • Protecting the interests of investors in securities
  • Promoting the development of the securities market
  • Regulating the business in stock exchanges and other securities markets
  • Registering and regulating various market intermediaries
  • Prohibiting fraudulent and unfair trade practices

Reserve Bank of India (RBI)

Regulates the government securities market and foreign exchange market, playing a complementary role in capital market regulation.

Key Roles:

  • Regulator of government securities market
  • Manager of public debt
  • Regulator of foreign exchange under FEMA
  • Overseer of payment and settlement systems

Key Market Participants

The capital market ecosystem consists of various intermediaries and participants:

Participant Role Examples
Stock Exchanges Provide platform for trading securities NSE, BSE
Depositories Hold securities in electronic form NSDL, CDSL
Brokers Execute trades on behalf of clients Zerodha, ICICI Direct
Investment Bankers Assist in raising capital Goldman Sachs, JM Financial
Mutual Funds Pool funds for investment HDFC MF, SBI MF
Foreign Institutional Investors Invest in Indian securities BlackRock, Vanguard

Recent Developments

The Indian capital market has witnessed significant reforms and innovations in recent years:

Key Developments:
  • Introduction of Direct Plan in Mutual Funds (2013)
  • Launch of REITs and InvITs (2014)
  • Implementation of T+1 settlement cycle (2023)
  • Introduction of Social Stock Exchange (2023)
  • Growing adoption of ESG investing
  • Rise of retail participation through digital platforms
