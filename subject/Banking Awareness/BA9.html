Regulations of Banks & NBFCs

Understanding the regulatory framework governing financial institutions in India

Regulatory Framework Overview

The Indian financial system is regulated by multiple authorities to ensure stability, protect consumer interests, and promote economic growth. Banks and Non-Banking Financial Companies (NBFCs) operate under different regulatory frameworks despite offering similar financial services.

Key Regulatory Bodies

Various institutions oversee the functioning of banks and NBFCs in India:

Reserve Bank of India (RBI)

The central banking institution responsible for regulating banks, NBFCs, and payment systems. It formulates monetary policy and maintains financial stability.

Ministry of Finance

Responsible for banking legislation, policy direction, and appointments to public sector banks. It oversees the implementation of major financial sector reforms.

Securities and Exchange Board of India (SEBI)

Regulates capital markets and protects investor interests. It oversees merchant banking activities of both banks and NBFCs.

Insurance Regulatory and Development Authority (IRDAI)

Regulates insurance companies and insurance-related activities of banks and NBFCs, including bancassurance partnerships.

National Housing Bank (NHB)

Regulates and supervises housing finance companies, which are a specialized category of NBFCs.

Credit Information Companies

Regulated by RBI, these companies (like CIBIL, Experian) maintain credit histories of borrowers for both banks and NBFCs.

Regulation of Banks

Banks in India operate under a comprehensive regulatory framework with strict compliance requirements:

Licensing Requirements

Banking Regulation Act, 1949

All banking companies must obtain a license from RBI under Section 22 of the Banking Regulation Act, 1949.

Minimum Capital Requirements

Scheduled commercial banks require minimum paid-up capital of ₹500 crores. Small Finance Banks require ₹200 crores.

Fit and Proper Criteria

Promoters and directors must meet RBI's "fit and proper" criteria based on integrity, reputation, and experience.

Prudential Regulations

Capital Adequacy

Banks must maintain minimum Capital to Risk-weighted Assets Ratio (CRAR) of 9% under Basel III norms (11.5% including capital conservation buffer).

Statutory Reserves

Banks must maintain Cash Reserve Ratio (CRR) and Statutory Liquidity Ratio (SLR) as prescribed by RBI.

NPA Classification

Strict asset classification norms: Standard (0-90 days), Sub-standard (90 days-12 months), Doubtful (12+ months), Loss assets.

Governance & Compliance

Board Composition

Banks must have a balanced board with adequate representation of independent directors and specific committees (Audit, Risk, Nomination).

Risk Management

Mandatory implementation of comprehensive risk management frameworks covering credit, market, operational, and liquidity risks.

Reporting Requirements

Banks must submit periodic returns to RBI covering financial position, asset quality, capital adequacy, and compliance status.

Consumer Protection

Fair Practices Code

Banks must adopt transparent practices in lending, recovery, and customer service as per RBI guidelines.

Banking Ombudsman

Customers can approach Banking Ombudsman for grievance redressal regarding deficient services.

Data Security

Banks must implement robust cybersecurity measures and data protection protocols as per RBI guidelines.

Regulation of NBFCs

NBFCs are regulated under a different framework with varying requirements based on their size, activity, and systemic importance:

Registration & Classification

Registration Requirements

NBFCs must register with RBI under Section 45-IA of RBI Act, 1934 with minimum net owned funds of ₹2 crores (₹10 crores for certain categories).

Classification System

NBFCs classified by:

  • Asset size (NBFCs, NBFCs-NDSI, NBFCs-D)
  • Activity (Investment, Loan, Infrastructure Finance, etc.)
  • Deposit taking ability (NBFC-D, NBFC-ND)

Scale-Based Regulation

Four-layer regulatory structure: Base Layer, Middle Layer, Upper Layer, and Top Layer with increasing regulatory intensity.

Prudential Norms

Capital Requirements

NBFCs must maintain minimum Tier I capital of 10% and CRAR of 15% (with some variations based on category).

Asset Classification

NPA classification: Standard (0-90 days), Sub-standard (90 days-12 months), Doubtful (12+ months), Loss assets. Provisioning requirements apply.

Concentration Norms

Exposure limits for single borrower (25% of capital funds) and group borrowers (40% of capital funds) with some exceptions.

Governance & Compliance

Corporate Governance

NBFCs must constitute Board committees, appoint Chief Risk Officer, and follow fit and proper criteria for directors.

Technology Governance

Mandatory IT governance framework, cybersecurity policy, and appointment of Chief Information Security Officer for larger NBFCs.

Reporting Framework

Regular submission of returns to RBI including financial statements, asset classification, and compliance certificates.

Deposit Acceptance

Deposit Taking NBFCs

NBFCs-D require specific credit rating, minimum NOF, and can accept public deposits up to 1.5 times net owned funds.

Deposit Insurance

Deposits with NBFCs-D are not insured by DICGC, unlike bank deposits which are insured up to ₹5 lakhs.

Restrictions

NBFCs cannot accept demand deposits and cannot offer interest rates higher than ceiling rates prescribed by RBI.

Key Differences in Regulation

While banks and NBFCs offer similar financial services, their regulatory treatment differs significantly:

Parameter Banks NBFCs
Main Regulating Act Banking Regulation Act, 1949 RBI Act, 1934
Demand Deposits Can accept Cannot accept
Payment System Membership Can be full members Limited access
Deposit Insurance Covered by DICGC up to ₹5 lakh Not covered

Evolution of Regulatory Framework

The regulatory approach towards banks and NBFCs has evolved significantly over time:

1934
RBI Act Enactment

Establishment of Reserve Bank of India with central banking functions.

1949
Banking Regulation Act

Comprehensive legislation for regulation of banking companies in India.

1963
First NBFC Directions

RBI issued first directions for NBFCs regarding acceptance of deposits.

1997
NBFC Comprehensive Regulation

RBI introduced comprehensive regulation for NBFCs including mandatory registration.

2006
Banking Ombudsman Expansion

Banking Ombudsman Scheme extended to cover NBFCs with certain criteria.

2014
Differentiated Banking

Introduction of Payments Banks and Small Finance Banks with specialized regulations.

2019
NBFC Liquidity Framework

Enhanced regulatory framework for NBFCs following IL&FS crisis, including liquidity risk management guidelines.

2021
Scale-Based Regulation

Introduction of four-layer regulatory structure for NBFCs based on size, activity, and riskiness.

Compliance Framework

Both banks and NBFCs must adhere to comprehensive compliance requirements:

KYC/AML Guidelines

Strict customer identification procedures, transaction monitoring, and reporting of suspicious transactions to FIU-IND.

Cyber Security Framework

Implementation of robust cybersecurity measures, incident reporting, and regular security audits.

Data Privacy

Compliance with data protection standards including RBI's data localization requirements for payment systems.

Fair Practices Code

Transparent lending practices, non-coercive recovery methods, and grievance redressal mechanisms.

Financial Reporting

Timely submission of audited financial statements and various regulatory returns to RBI.

Regulatory Inspections

Regular on-site inspections by RBI with focus on governance, risk management, and compliance culture.
