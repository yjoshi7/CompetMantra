Banking Awareness

Basel Norms (I, II, III)

International regulatory framework for banks to mitigate financial and operational risks

Understanding Basel Accords

The Basel Accords are a set of recommendations on banking laws and regulations issued by the Basel Committee on Banking Supervision (BCBS). They provide a framework for risk management and promote stability in the international financial system.

Evolution of Basel Framework

1988

Basel I

Focused on credit risk and minimum capital requirements

2004

Basel II

Three pillars approach covering wider risks

2010-2017

Basel III

Enhanced regulation post 2008 financial crisis

Basel I (1988)

The first Basel Accord, introduced in 1988, focused primarily on credit risk and established a minimum capital requirement of 8% of risk-weighted assets.

Key Focus

Credit risk mitigation through minimum capital requirements

Capital Calculation

Minimum Capital to Risk-weighted Assets Ratio (CRAR) of 8%

Risk Weights

Assets classified into five risk categories (0%, 10%, 20%, 50%, 100%)

Limitations

Ignored operational risk, over-simplified credit risk, encouraged regulatory arbitrage

Basel II (2004)

Introduced in 2004, Basel II built upon Basel I with a three-pillar approach that addressed additional risk types and encouraged better risk management practices.

Pillar 1: Minimum Capital Requirements

Enhanced calculation of credit, market, and operational risk capital requirements

  • Standardized Approach
  • Foundation IRB Approach
  • Advanced IRB Approach

Pillar 2: Supervisory Review

Regulators ensure banks have adequate capital to cover all risks

  • Internal Capital Adequacy Assessment Process (ICAAP)
  • Supervisory review and evaluation
  • Intervention when needed

Pillar 3: Market Discipline

Enhanced disclosure requirements to promote transparency

  • Capital structure disclosure
  • Risk exposure assessment
  • Capital adequacy information

Basel III (2010-2017)

Developed in response to the deficiencies in financial regulation revealed by the 2008 financial crisis, Basel III strengthens bank capital requirements and introduces new regulatory requirements on bank liquidity and leverage.

4.5%
Common Equity Tier 1 (CET1) Capital Ratio
6.0%
Tier 1 Capital Ratio
8.0%
Total Capital Ratio
2.5%
Capital Conservation Buffer

Liquidity Requirements

Introduced Liquidity Coverage Ratio (LCR) and Net Stable Funding Ratio (NSFR) to ensure short-term and long-term liquidity

Leverage Ratio

Non-risk-based leverage ratio of 3% to restrict excessive leverage in the banking system

Countercyclical Buffer

0-2.5% buffer that can be implemented during periods of high credit growth

Comparison of Basel Norms

Basel I

  • Introduced in 1988
  • Focused only on credit risk
  • Minimum capital requirement: 8%
  • Simple risk weighting
  • No supervisory review
  • No market discipline

Basel II

  • Introduced in 2004
  • Covered credit, market, operational risks
  • Three pillar approach
  • Advanced risk measurement
  • Supervisory review process
  • Market discipline through disclosure

Basel III

  • Introduced in 2010-2017
  • Enhanced capital requirements
  • Leverage ratio introduced
  • Liquidity requirements (LCR, NSFR)
  • Capital conservation buffer
  • Countercyclical buffer

Implementation in India

The Reserve Bank of India (RBI) has implemented Basel norms in a phased manner, considering the specific needs of the Indian banking system.

Basel Norm Implementation Timeline Key Features Implemented
Basel I 1992-1993 Minimum 8% CRAR for banks
Basel II 2007-2009 Three pillars approach, standardized approach for risk measurement
Basel III 2013-2019 Higher capital ratios, capital conservation buffer, leverage ratio, LCR, NSFR

Key Takeaways for Indian Banking System

  • Public sector banks faced challenges in meeting capital requirements
  • RBI introduced differentiated banking structure to address implementation issues
  • Indian banks generally maintained higher capital ratios than Basel minimums
  • Implementation helped Indian banks remain resilient during global financial crises
