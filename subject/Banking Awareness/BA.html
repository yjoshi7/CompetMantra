History & Evolution of Banking in India

What is a Bank?

A bank is a financial institution that accepts money from the public in the form of deposits and lends money in the form of loans. Banks play a crucial role in the economic development of a country by mobilizing savings and facilitating investments.

Banking Evolution Timeline

Pre-Independence Era (1770-1947)

1683
Bank of Madras Origins
  • The Carnatic Bank (1788), the British Bank of Madras (1795), and the Asiatic Bank (1804) eventually amalgamated to form the Bank of Madras in 1843
  • It was restructured as one of the presidency banks in 1843
1786
The General Bank of India
  • Founded in 1786 in Calcutta
  • Faced challenges and ceased operations in 1791
1770
The Bank of Hindustan
  • Established by Alexander and Company
  • First modern private bank to issue paper currency notes
  • Closed in 1832 due to recession in 1819 and banking crisis in 1832
1806
Bank of Calcutta

Established in 1806 and renamed as Bank of Bengal in 1809

1840
Bank of Bombay

Established in 1840

1843
Bank of Madras

Established in 1843

1921
Imperial Bank

The three presidency banks (Bank of Bengal, Bank of Bombay, and Bank of Madras) were merged to form Imperial Bank

1935
Reserve Bank of India
  • RBI Act was proposed in assembly in 1926 by Hilton Young Commission (Royal Commission)
  • RBI was established on April 1, 1935 under RBI Act 1934

Post-Independence Era (1947-Present)

1949
RBI Nationalization
  • RBI was nationalized in 1949 and came under government control
  • Reasons included post-independence control, aligning with national goals, preventing private dominance, and regulating banking system
1955
State Bank of India

Imperial Bank of India was converted to State Bank of India (SBI)

1969
First Bank Nationalization

14 major private commercial banks were nationalized by Prime Minister Indira Gandhi

1980
Second Bank Nationalization

Another 6 commercial banks were nationalized

1990s
Liberalization Era
  • The 1991 economic crisis forced India to liberalize under Narasimha Committee suggestions
  • Birth of new private banks: HDFC, ICICI, and Axis Bank

The Story of Indian Banking

Chapter 1: The Barter Trouble

Once upon a time (long before money existed)...

  • People traded cows for rice, pots for cloth—this was called barter.
  • Problem: What if you had rice but needed shoes, but the shoemaker wanted milk? Too confusing!

Solution?

  • Silver coins came, then paper money—but who kept it safe?

Chapter 2: The First Indian "Bankers" – Sethji & Shroff Uncle

  • Sethji (a rich merchant) and Shroff Uncle (a moneylender) helped people:
    • Stored gold for safekeeping.
    • Lent money (but charged high interest!).
  • Hundis (like today's cheques) were used for long-distance trade.

But…

  • No rules! If Sethji ran away, your money was gone. 😱

Chapter 3: The British Arrive – East India Company (EIC) & Agency Houses

Year: 1600s

  • EIC (East India Company) came to trade spices, silk, and tea.
  • Problem: They needed loans to buy goods and fight wars.

Enter the Agency Houses (1770s):

  • Like "British Sethjis"—they traded AND lent money.
  • Bank of Hindustan (1770): First modern bank! But…
    • Closed in 1832 because they took too many risks (like gambling all money on opium trade!).

Chapter 4: The Three Banking Heroes – Presidency Banks!

EIC thought: "We need SAFE banks!"

So, they created:

  1. Bank of Calcutta (1806) → Renamed Bank of Bengal (1809)
  2. Bank of Bombay (1840)
  3. Bank of Madras (1843)

How They Helped:

  • Held government money.
  • Gave loans to traders.
  • Printed paper currency (like RBI today!).

Fun Fact: These three later merged into Imperial Bank (1921), which became SBI!

Chapter 5: RBI – The Superhero of Banks (1935)

Problem: Banks kept failing! No one controlled them.

Solution:

  • Hilton Young Commission (1926) suggested a "Bank of Banks".
  • RBI was born in 1935!
    • Like a strict teacher—made sure banks didn't cheat.
    • Printed money, controlled loans.

But… RBI was private-owned (by British!). After 1947, India said: "Now it's OURS!" → Nationalized in 1949.

Chapter 6: After Freedom – Big Changes!

  1. 1949: RBI nationalized – now under Indian government.
  2. 1955: Imperial Bank became State Bank of India (SBI).
  3. 1969: Indira Gandhi nationalized 14 big banks (so rich people couldn't control them!).
  4. 1991: New private banks opened (HDFC, ICICI) – more competition!

Timeline Recap (Easy to Remember!)

Final Lesson: Why Does This Matter?

  • Before: Money was unsafe, no rules.
  • Now: Banks keep money safe, help businesses grow, and RBI watches over all!
